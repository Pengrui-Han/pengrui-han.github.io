How Does the Mind Work?

The mind doesn’t read reality off the data — it bets on it. So does every learning system we build.

By Pengrui Han · December 2025 · ~7 min read

The mind doesn’t read reality off the data — it constructs it.

You have never seen the 3D world. You have seen a 2D image projected onto your retina, and your brain has made a fast, unconscious bet about what produced it. That bet is so good and so silent that it feels like seeing. It isn’t. It’s inference.

Spending time with the cognitive science literature, I’ve come to think this — the betting — is the central operation the mind performs. Whether the problem is recovering a 3D scene from a flat image, binding scattered features into a coherent object, or predicting the future from a handful of examples, the underlying shape is the same: solve an ill-posed problem by leaning on constraints that the data itself does not supply.

I’m an LLM researcher. I spend most of my time thinking about what neural networks do well and where they fail. Reading cognitive science, I keep feeling the same shape underneath both. So here are the notes I’ve been keeping, with the bridge to AI written into the margins.

The mind as software

To understand how the mind works, we need to distinguish it from the brain. The brain is the physical hardware; the mind is the software running on it. That software is a collection of heuristic algorithms designed and refined to solve problems.

The central challenge the mind faces is that most problems of survival are ill-posed: the data we receive from the world is incomplete, ambiguous, or sometimes essentially unsolvable from the input alone. So the mind survives by exploiting constraints and innate assumptions about the physical world to make intelligent guesses. This strategy runs all the way up the hierarchy of interaction with the world — from perceiving space, to binding distinct features into objects, to reasoning about what happens next.

Perception: inverse optics

We see this most cleanly at the level of perception, in what cognitive scientists call inverse optics. The visual system tries to recover a 3D world from a 2D retinal image — a mathematically underdetermined problem, because infinitely many 3D worlds could produce the same image. To solve it, the mind applies the non-accidentalness assumption. If it perceives a straight line, it assumes the edge in the world is truly straight, rejecting the unlikely viewing coincidences that could have produced the same line from a curved edge seen from just the right angle.

By betting that features are real rather than accidental, the mind constructs a coherent spatial reality from input that doesn’t logically force any single interpretation. Perception, in this view, is not measurement. It is the most plausible interpretation of ambiguous data.

Attention: binding

One level up, the mind has to extract stable objects from a flood of partial features that don’t come pre-labeled as belonging together. Different tasks demand different combinations of features, but the mind must extract invariance and produce coherent, unified objects under tight resource constraints. The mechanism is attention as binding: it connects the relevant features into a single object file.

We know this is constructive — not given by the input — because of how it fails. When attention is overloaded, the mind makes illusory conjunctions: it stitches features into objects that do not exist. A red triangle and a blue circle in a fast display can be remembered as a blue triangle and a red circle. The sensory input never specified which feature belonged to which object; the mind had to construct that mapping, and under load, it constructs the wrong one.

Reasoning: induction

The same challenge appears at the top of the hierarchy. The data we observe is rarely enough to deductively secure the right conclusion. The mind closes the gap through induction. Observing a thousand black ravens does not prove the next one will not be white; infinitely many logically consistent hypotheses fit the same data. (Goodman’s famous example: maybe all ravens are grue — green before the year 3000, blue after.)

The mind resolves this by leaning on constraints that favor projectible predicates. We treat properties like “blackness” as stable features of the bird, not temporary accidents bound to a particular time slice. This is not derivable from observation. It is a prior the mind brings — and it is what lets us predict the future despite the logical gap.

The bridge to AI

Once you see this pattern, it is hard to unsee it in machine learning. Every learning system that generalizes does so by encoding inductive biases — assumptions about which hypotheses are plausible, baked into architecture, objective, or training data. Convolutional networks work because translation invariance is the right non-accidentalness assumption for natural images. The role transformer attention plays in routing information bears a structural family resemblance to attention’s binding role in cognition, though it’s used differently. And LLM generalization failures often look like Goodman’s grue problem in disguise: the model has fit the data, but it has bet on the wrong projectible predicate, and a tiny perturbation reveals it.

The cognitive scientists got here first, by a few decades. They had to: their organisms had to survive the ill-posed world before anyone wrote a backprop equation. The takeaway, for someone like me, is not that AI is the same as the mind — it isn’t. The takeaway is that the shape of the problem is the same. Neither system has enough data to deduce the right answer. Both have to lean on something the data does not say. The interesting question, in both cases, is which constraints they lean on and why.

In a world where the available information is ambiguous and many underlying problems are ill-posed, the mind cannot simply read reality off the data. It has to bet — and the structure of those bets is what makes coherent perception, stable objects, and meaningful reasoning possible at all.

I find this both humbling and energizing. Humbling, because the field I work in has been wrestling with the same fundamental problem for far less time than the brain has. Energizing, because the cognitive science vocabulary — ill-posed, non-accidental, binding, projectible — gives us sharper tools to ask what our models are actually doing when they generalize, and sharper questions to ask when they fail.
